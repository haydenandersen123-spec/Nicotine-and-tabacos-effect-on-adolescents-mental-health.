Investigating the Causal Relationship Between Nicotine and Tobacco’s Effect on Adolescents' Mental Health

Introduction

Tobacco usage, specifically, by nicotine dependency, is among one of the most preventable causes of illness as well as death worldwide. Although numerous public wellness warnings have been shown, tobacco products continue to appeal to teenagers with social pressure, tension break beliefs, as well as new nicotine devices such as e-cigarettes as well as vapes. Teenagers, whose brains continue to develop, continue to be most susceptible to nicotine's addictive potential as well as mental illness effects of nicotine. This research proposal describes research examining the causal relationship between nicotine as well as tobacco usage as well as mental illness in teenagers, specifically the neurobiological effects, worsening of mental illness, as well as late consequences of pre-exposure. Ideally, this research establishes if the negative outcomes of teenager tobacco usage for mental illness outweighs all potential benefits, as well as informs future public wellness interventions.

Background

Tobacco's prevalence in adolescence is a worldwide issue. Tobacco causes the deaths of over 8 million people annually, with numerous people starting the habit in adolescence (WHO, 2020). In Australia, the onset of smoking in 14- to 17-year-old adolescents is at around 5%, although e-cigarette prevalence has increased (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare [AIHW], 2021). Adolescents experience accelerated maturation of the brain, particularly in the prefrontal cortex, the center of decision-making, emotional experience, as well as the brain location for the highest concentration of nicotine receptors (Yuan et al., 2015). Exposure to nicotine during this time damages neurological circuits, putting teenagers at risk for mood swings, depression, as well as anxiety disorders (Goriounova & Mansvelder, 2012).

Nicotine has action at nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs), exciting the release of dopamine and the reinforcement of addictive behavior (Benowitz, 2010). Chronic nicotine administration can also dysregulate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, further exacerbating psychological morbidity (Torres & O'Dell, 2016). While most adolescents start smoking to manage stress or depression, the resulting dependence typically exacerbates these conditions in the long term (Lechner et al., 2017).

New nicotine delivery tools like vapes and e-cigarettes came with myths about safety. While promoted as quit tools, the tools contain more nicotine than cigarettes, which affects teen brain development in negative ways (Yuan et al., 2015). Normalization of vaping in the classroom as well as in friends increases the reach of these substances as well as represses perceived harm, further blurring prevention messages. Social networking sites popularize vaping as well, presenting it as this season's trend as well as not as the health risk it is, consequently generating peer pressure as well as experimentation (Primack et al., 2018). This has increased the formation of nicotine dependency at young ages as well as increased the challenge of quitting among teenagers.

Moreover, the psychological impact of tobacco in adolescents can go un-treated by reason of stigma or restricted therapy for psychoses. Adolescents with nervousness or depression can choose against going for professionals, but rather self-medicating with nicotine. This aberrant behavior strengthens the habit cycle, hence the permanent psychopathological disability. Stressful moments of academic transition, social maturation, as well as the formulation of identity, make teenagers vulnerable to the abuse of nicotine products as trials in self-medication.

Worsening the issue is the lack of comprehensive education about the long-term neurological effects of nicotine in youth. Teachers, parents, even medical professionals themselves can best limit the risk of nicotine abuse in adolescents, discussing the subject in isolation of the risk for lung or heart diseases without consideration of the effect on cognitive abilities as well as moods. This lack of education and communication allows harmful modes of usage to go unimpeded, even as medical evidence continues to mount for mental health downturns.

Objectives of the Study

The study seeks to determine whether there exists causation between the consumption of tobacco or nicotine and the rising mental illness among minors. This study shall discuss the relationship between nicotine usage and the presence of depression, as well as anxiety, as well as mood conditions. Another objective is to discuss evidence of the therapeutic nicotine effect as well as determine whether the benefit falls on the minor's part, thus out shadowing the risk implication.

Hypothesis

The research hypothesis is that cigarettes and nicotine damage the mental health of teenagers, accelerating the rates of mental illness like depression and nervousness. All of the therapeutic or cognitive advantages of nicotine are counteracted by nicotine's debilitating impact upon the growth, as well as the mental, of teenagers.

Research Methods

The research is going to adopt the mixed-methods design, predominantly quantitative, in the analysis of the secondary national health surveys, peer articles, as well as the neuroscientific studies. Data is going to be sourced from the AIHW, the National Drug Strategy Household Survey (NDSHS), as well as the Young Minds Matter, all in the past 15 years.

The participants will be adolescents between the ages of 14–17 years. Variables of interest in the study include the onset age, the nicotine usage frequency, the product usage type (conventional cigarette vs. e-cigarette), and co-existence of mental illness disorders. Neurobiological research findings will also undergo evaluation as regards the effects of nicotine upon the brain structure as well as the function of neurotransmission.

Thorough literature review would involve meta-analysis, cohort studies, as well as neuroscientific studies that track the growth of the brain, addictive behavior dynamics, and mental health outcomes. Individual case studies can further include qualitative interviews for the construction of personal accounts of adolescent nicotine users as well as the effect it has had on mental health.

Acquisition of Data

The secondary data is to be extracted from the datasets provided in the public domains as well as the peer journals. Neuroscientific data is to be retrieved from journals like Neuropsychopharmacology, Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Medicine, as well as from the Journal of Physiology.

No ethical clearance is needed for the present proposal since it is based on existing, de-identified datasets. However, the research shall comply with ethical data interpretation as well as reportability. Transparency in methodological approach as well as in citation practices shall be observed in order to maintain integrity.

Statistical Analysis

Statistical analysis will include the computation of Pearson's measures of correlation in extending the analysis of the relationship between nicotine consumption and mental health outcomes. T-tests will establish the presence of observable differences in the psychological wellbeing of nicotine users as well as non-users of nicotine. Logistic regression will establish nicotine as the independent variable adjusting for confounders of socio-economic standing, mental illness in the family, as well as the availability of mental health care.

Biological endpoints shall be examined by combining studies using MRI scan, animal studies, and post-mortem analysis of the brain to follow the effects of nicotine on brain maturation as well as emotional regulation. Such information shall be qualitatively combined in order to inform quantitative results. Combination of cross-sectional as well as longitudinal research studies shall facilitate identification of instantaneous as well as long-term effects. Sensitivity analysis shall be undertaken for the purposes of ascertaining the results for robustness as well as for compensating for the weaknesses in the secondary data.

Counter-Argument

Although nicotine is detrimental when used recreationally, particularly in adolescence, it has demonstrated potential therapeutic value in regulated clinical trials. Scientific studies indicate that nicotine enhances attention, working memory, and cognitive performance in adults, specifically in the treatment of neurological illness. For instance, trials conducted by Newhouse et al. (2011) and Levin et al. (1996) demonstrated that the administration of nicotine enhanced the cognitive abilities of patients with Alzheimer’s illness as well as schizophrenia, respectively. Nicotine has further been examined as part of the treatment of Parkinson’s illness as a potential neuroprotective compound in virtue of the anti-inflammatory properties of the compound as well as to modulate the neurotransmission systems (Quik et al., 2012). Such evidence indicates the therapeutic potential of nicotine or similar molecules in the treatment of specific cognitive as well as neurological illnesses. Nonetheless, it is crucial to recognize that these therapeutic applications arise in the context of stringent medical supervision, which is in no way transferrable to adolescent experimentation, in which the risk far surpasses the potential benefit.

Conclusions

Wider studies to date suggest high nicotine use in adolescence is linked to worsening mental health. Lechner et al. (2017) found nicotine users in adolescence had depression symptoms twice as high as non-users had. Logistic Regression identified nicotine exposure specifically as, in the absence of the presence of other variables, able to predict adverse mental health outcomes.

Primack et al. (2018) found e-cigarette users had higher levels of suicidal ideation. Yuan et al. (2015) as well as Goriounova & Mansvelder (2012) revealed nicotine triggers impulse control as well as emotional regulatory neural circuitries, leading to the formation of psychiatric symptoms. In some of the studies, it was found Pearson’s r is about -0.6, showing that at the rank order scale, there is a strong reverse relationship, inversely, in between mental health steadiness as well as nicotine consumption. Also, longitudinal evidence in the last few years (AIHW, 2021) verifies that the beginning of nicotine has a correlation with chronic psychological unhappiness in adulthood.
